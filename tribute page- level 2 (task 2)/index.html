Swami Vivekananda

Biography

Swami Vivekananda, born Narendranath Datta, was a prominent spiritual leader and philosopher of India in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. He was born on January 12, 1863, in Kolkata, India.

At a young age, Swami Vivekananda was deeply influenced by the teachings of his guru, Sri Ramakrishna Paramahamsa. He played a key role in the revival of Hinduism and the spread of Vedanta philosophy in the West.

Swami Vivekananda is best known for his historic speech at the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago in 1893, where he introduced Hinduism and Vedanta to the Western audience. His speech began with the famous words, "Sisters and brothers of America," and received a standing ovation.

Throughout his life, Swami Vivekananda emphasized the importance of self-realization, service to humanity, and the unity of all religions. He founded the Ramakrishna Math and the Ramakrishna Mission, organizations dedicated to social service, education, and spiritual upliftment.

Significant Contributions

  • Introduction of Hinduism and Vedanta to the Western world
  • Establishment of the Ramakrishna Math and Ramakrishna Mission
  • Promotion of education and service through various missions and institutions
  • Advocacy for religious tolerance, harmony, and universal brotherhood

Quotes and Lectures

"Arise, awake, and stop not until the goal is achieved."

"In a conflict between the heart and the brain, follow your heart."

"The greatest religion is to be true to your own nature. Have faith in yourselves."
